Eric Monday

City Attorney → Dual-Role Assistant City Manager → Terminated January 2023

A city attorney who was also made assistant city manager — a dual role flagged as a conflict of interest at the time — whose contract was quietly altered by an outgoing council in its final weeks, then terminated by the council that replaced it. He said he wouldn't seek severance. He later sought more than a million dollars anyway.

The Dual Role The Contract Alteration and Termination — 2022–2023 The Severance Dispute — 2023–2025
Era One
The Dual Role

Monday served as Martinsville's City Attorney before then-City Manager Leon Towarnicki hired him into a second, simultaneous role as Assistant City Manager — holding both positions at once. Outspoken former Councilman Danny Turner was among those who considered the arrangement a conflict of interest at the time, given that a city's own attorney advising council and a city manager's own assistant are meant to serve, structurally, somewhat different functions.

What The Record Shows

This dual-role arrangement is the direct reason the city later changed its own charter — a reform documented in Era Three below. Whatever else is disputed about Monday's departure, the structural problem his case exposed was real enough that the city moved to make it impossible for anyone to repeat.

Era Two
The Contract Alteration and Termination
November 2022 – January 2023

Documents released under the Virginia Freedom of Information Act show that Monday's employment contract was altered by the outgoing council majority after the November 2022 election — the same council that included Jennifer Bowles, in her final weeks as Vice Mayor. The alteration gave Monday full credit for years he had spent working only part-time for the city, a change that would later inflate what he was entitled to claim in severance.

Bowles's own last act as outgoing Vice Mayor, at the December 13, 2022 meeting, was the motion that carried to terminate Monday's contract effective January 2, 2023 — explicitly framed by Bowles at the time as not about any wrongdoing, but to let the city "go in a new direction." The new council, seated after the November 2022 election (Jones and Rawls among them), inherited a contract that had just been altered in Monday's favor days or weeks before his termination took effect.

Open Question

Whether the outgoing council's contract alteration and Bowles's own termination motion were coordinated, sequential parts of the same plan, or two separate actions by the same outgoing body acting on different timelines, isn't established in any source reviewed. Both are documented facts; the relationship between them is not.

Era Three
The Severance Dispute
2023 – October 2025

Monday initially told council members, in open session, that he did not intend to seek a $100,000 severance when his revised contract was terminated. That position did not hold. He later pursued retirement benefits the Bulletin reported as $1 million or more, in a dispute stemming directly from the 2023 termination and the contract terms altered just before it.

"The current council has been able to resolve one of the several contractual disputes that arose under the prior administration as a result of actions by certain elected city officials and employees... As we move forward to solidify and strengthen the city with fiscal and policy best practices, it is our belief that this settlement of $110,000 is in the best interest of our community."
— Councilman Lawrence Mitchell, reading a prepared council statement, Tuesday night vote

The council voted unanimously to settle with Monday for $110,000 — described by a council member to the Bulletin as representing one year's salary, a small fraction of the seven-figure claim Monday had pursued.

The Reform That Followed

The same council statement framed the settlement as one of several steps "to protect the citizens of Martinsville," specifically citing "approved changes to the charter to prevent serving as a dual employee, both as a city council appointee and as an administrative employee" — a direct, named response to the exact arrangement Monday had held under Towarnicki.

What The Record Shows

Monday's case sits at the intersection of two separate threads already documented elsewhere in this record: the outgoing-council contract alteration ties directly to Bowles's final weeks in office, and the case itself produced one of the few concrete structural reforms on record — a charter change specifically barring the dual-employee arrangement that made his case possible in the first place. The escalation from "no severance sought" to a seven-figure claim, settled for roughly a tenth of that amount, is documented here without further characterization; the gap between those two positions speaks for itself.
