Person Record · Full Arc

Rico Johnson

Owner, Kornna Kitchen (Uptown Martinsville, 2022–2025) & Kafe Da'Ville (Danville, 2025–Present)

A restaurateur who once stood before City Council asking the police chief to help protect his newly licensed business is now the plaintiff in a $2 million lawsuit against that same chief. In between: two felony arrests four days apart, twenty total counts, an on-camera statement calling his treatment "a racial issue," and a relaunch sixty miles north. This page documents the full arc — the cooperative years, the crisis, and the lawsuit that followed.

Era One
The Restaurant
November 2022 – October 2024

Johnson, originally from Washington, D.C., became a master barber by 17 and owned his first barbershop at 19. He later ran a hot dog stand business out of the Foggy Bottom Metro station starting in 1994, sold it in 1997, and grew the proceeds into ownership of 15 tractor-trailers. He holds a business degree from Howard University and is the father of five. His recipes — and the restaurant's name — trace to his mother, Paulette Smith-Duckett, whom he has called his best friend and closest influence.

From Henry County To Uptown

Johnson opened the original Kornna Kitchen at 41 Stones Dairy Road in Henry County around Thanksgiving 2022. By September 2023, he had relocated to uptown Martinsville, taking over the former Shindig Restaurant space at 37 East Main Street. He described his goal as building a "community restaurant," framing the move explicitly around the idea that the space wasn't his alone — it belonged to the city.

Open Question

Johnson filed for Chapter 13 bankruptcy in U.S. Bankruptcy Court in Greensboro, North Carolina, on February 1, 2023 — roughly seven months before the uptown relocation. A scheduled creditors' meeting was postponed twice (to September 23, then October 28, 2023), and the outcome has not been confirmed in any source reviewed so far. Whether the bankruptcy and the uptown expansion were connected, or simply overlapped in time, isn't established.

Free Lunches And A Council Ally

Through 2024, Johnson built a visible record of civic goodwill: free lunches for city employees, teachers, bus drivers, and local charities, framed by Johnson as part of the city's broader "evolution." On August 14, 2024, he appeared before City Council to request stricter anti-loitering regulations near the restaurant, citing a recent incident in which an officer and a suspect crashed through a plate-glass window during a crowd-dispersal effort on Main Street, injuring both. In the same appearance, Johnson personally appealed to Police Chief Robert Fincher not to close Main Street to traffic, explaining that doing so could hurt the alcohol business he'd just gained the right to operate under a newly acquired ABC license.

"We need laws that protect our businesses and ensure the safety of our community."
— Rico Johnson, Martinsville City Council, August 14, 2024

Two weeks later, on August 31, 2024, Johnson hosted a "Seafood & Music Block Party" outside the restaurant, with city crews visibly assisting in street preparation — barricades, cones, and equipment trucks documented moving into place along East Main Street. The post drew 194 reactions, 57 comments, and 29 shares, among the highest engagement of any Kornna Kitchen-related coverage reviewed.

A Separate Controversy

At some point during this same general period — the exact date is not yet confirmed — Johnson faced a different and unrelated controversy: an accusation, conveyed to him by message, that the restaurant did not treat white customers fairly. Johnson responded at length in a live Facebook video from the restaurant, describing his staff as six unpaid volunteers working long hours and framing the restaurant's mission around serving everyone regardless of background. The video drew 126 supportive comments.

Open Question

This incident is distinct from the racial-issue statement Johnson would later make about the city itself (Era Three) and from the racial-discrimination framing in his 2025 lawsuit (Era Four). The three should not be merged into a single narrative — they involve different accusers, different directions, and, as far as the record shows, no stated connection between them.

Era Two
The Arrests
November 2024

Just before 1 a.m. on Saturday, November 23, 2024, a man named Fulcher and a woman identified in court filings only as Ms. Hall — unrelated to then-Commonwealth's Attorney Andy Hall — walked across the street to Kornna Kitchen to speak with Johnson about comments he'd made on Facebook Live. According to Martinsville Police Officer Ryan O'Hara's later courtroom testimony, Johnson's response, made both in person and live on the restaurant's Facebook page, escalated quickly.

"He told them 'I'll drop you dead' multiple times and said, 'Your days are numbered,' and 'I've got my gun.'"
— Officer Ryan O'Hara, testifying in Martinsville General District Court, on Johnson's recorded statements

The entire exchange was captured on video and shared on Kornna Kitchen's own Facebook page. Johnson was arrested on two felony counts of threats of death or bodily injury. Four days later, on November 27, 2024, he was arrested again — this time on nine felony counts of obtaining money by false pretense and nine felony counts of larceny by bad check, each tied to amounts of $1,000 or more, with alleged incidents spanning June 20, 24, and 25; September 24; October 2 (twice); October 4; October 9; and October 18.

The financial counts were tied to insufficient-funds checks alleged to total more than $40,000 owed to food vendors. The case moved through the court process over the following months: certified to circuit court on June 12, 2025, indicted by a grand jury on July 28, 2025, and resolved at arraignment with a not-guilty plea.

What The Record Shows

The uptown area around Kornna Kitchen had independently seen a rise in violent incidents in the months before these arrests — two unrelated beatings, gunshots fired nearby, and the August 14 plate-glass window injury documented in Era One. None of that activity is tied to Johnson personally; it's separate context about the surrounding blocks, not evidence bearing on his own charges. The two arrests below are documented entirely on their own terms.

Era Three
The Reckoning
December 2024 – May 2025

At some point following the arrests, Johnson returned to a Martinsville City Council meeting and addressed members directly in a Star News TV segment titled "Rico Johnson Wants Answers From The City of Martinsville," which drew 91 reactions, 80 comments, and 21 shares. In it, Johnson made the most direct public statement of his entire documented arc:

"It is a racial issue here in Martinsville, it is definitely a racial issue, here in Martinsville."
— Rico Johnson, Martinsville City Council, "Wants Answers" segment
Open Question

The exact date of this statement is not yet confirmed, and Johnson did not specify on camera what particular treatment he was calling a racial issue — whether the loitering dispute, the building departure described below, the arrests themselves, or something else entirely. Logged precisely as stated, with no characterization added about what he was referencing.

By May 12, 2025, Johnson had vacated the 37 East Main Street buildings. Star News TV reported the restaurant's Facebook page then listed it as "temporarily closed," and quoted the building's owner, Will Pearson, offering his own account of why.

Why The Restaurant Closed — Two Framings

Building owner Will Pearson's account (May 12, 2025): Pearson said Johnson wanted to leave — framing the departure as Johnson's own choice.

Later court-case framing (October 2025 Bulletin coverage): Subsequent reporting on Johnson's lawsuit refers more simply to Kornna Kitchen having "closed" in May, without attributing the decision to either side. The record does not yet resolve whether the departure was voluntary, pressured, or some combination — both framings are shown rather than one chosen.

Two weeks later, on May 26–27, 2025, Johnson resurfaced sixty miles north. A Kafe Da'Ville Facebook page went live with Johnson on camera, and Star News TV confirmed the relaunch with a short post: "Rico IS BACK!" The name is a direct play on "Café Danville."

Era Four
The Lawsuit
October 2025 – Present

On October 12, 2025, Johnson filed a $2 million defamation lawsuit against Interim City Manager Robert Fincher and Council Member Aaron Rawls. The suit alleges their public comments about him caused injury to both his personal and business reputation — "humiliation, shame, vilification, exposure to public infamy, scandal, and disgrace," in its own language — while also framing Kornna Kitchen's closure as a consequence of that harm. It frames the restaurant explicitly through Johnson's own racial identity, describing him, as an African-American business owner, building a place meant to welcome everyone.

  • This case is separate from, and not legally connected to, the June 9, 2026 federal lawsuits involving Ferrell-Benavides and the broader governance crisis — different court, different plaintiff, different defendants
Source: JOHNSON-BULLETIN — Martinsville Bulletin, confirmed in hand

The lawsuit's timing creates a documented irony: an undated letter Fincher had previously written to the Virginia Alcoholic Beverage Control Authority described Kornna Kitchen as going "beyond just being a fine restaurant" — language that reads, in hindsight, as supportive of the same business Fincher is now being sued over. Whether that letter predates or postdates the souring of the relationship isn't established, and it should be read with that caution rather than as proof of any specific motive on Fincher's part.

Both defendants responded to the suit directly and on the record. Fincher drew a sharp line around his own statements:

"All actions and allegations concern my actions as the police chief... truth is a complete defense against defamation."
— Robert Fincher, responding to Johnson's lawsuit, October 2025

Rawls responded more dismissively.

"One would think the last place this guy wants to be is back in a courtroom after being indicted on felony charges, including larceny and threatening the lives of Martinsville citizens on video. Rico... ruined his own business. It's time for him to act his age and take accountability for his bad behavior."
— Aaron Rawls, on Johnson's lawsuit, October 2025
What The Record Shows

The same restaurateur who once asked Chief Fincher, in person, to help protect his liquor license from a street closure is now suing Fincher for allegedly destroying that same business. Whatever the merits of the underlying claims, the relationship's full arc — cooperative appeal, then lawsuit — is itself part of the record. As of this writing, the case remains active with no trial date or resolution confirmed.
