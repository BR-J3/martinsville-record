The Petition

Signature Drive · Three-Way Threshold Dispute · Judge Brinks Rules

In December 2025, Martinsville residents began a formal petition drive to remove Mayor LC Jones from office under Virginia law. Three officials cited three different signature thresholds — each based on a different interpretation of which election, and which votes, counted. The petition collected more than 400 valid signatures. The court ruled that was far short of what the law actually required.

The Record

Virginia law permits citizens to petition for the removal of a local elected official from office. The process requires collecting a specified number of verified signatures from registered voters, submitting them to the Commonwealth's Attorney, and — if the CA certifies the petition meets legal standards — presenting it to the circuit court for a hearing.

In December 2025, a group of Martinsville residents began that process against Mayor LC Jones. They cited concerns about city governance and transparency. What became equally documented was the dispute over how many signatures they needed — a number that three different officials calculated three different ways, using three different interpretations of the underlying election data.

The Three-Way Threshold Dispute

Petitioners ultimately gathered 495 signatures. The registrar's office certified 401 as valid. Whether that was enough depended entirely on which of three numbers applied.

Three Officials — Three Numbers — One Court Ruling
Sara Workman
Registrar 375 2022 election: 3,749 residential voters × 10%
Andy Hall
Former Commonwealth's Attorney 571 November 2024 turnout, 5,708 voters × 10%
Judge Marcus Brinks
Martinsville Circuit Court 682 6,818 total votes cast in the 2022 election × 10%
What The Record Shows

The 401 certified signatures cleared Workman's 375 by 26, fell 170 short of Hall's 571, and fell 281 short of the court's ruling of 682. The dispute traced back to which election applied, and what was being counted within it. Workman's 375 and the court's 682 both rest on the same election — the 2022 race that put Jones in office — but count different things. Workman counted the 3,749 residential voters who actually turned out. The court instead used the Virginia Elections Database's total of 6,818 recorded votes for that race, since the 2022 ballot allowed each voter to select up to two candidates for the seats up that year — meaning total votes cast runs well above the number of people who voted. Krudys, representing Jones, argued for the 682 figure on that basis, and the special prosecutor, Collins, conceded the point in court. Judge Brinks ruled accordingly. Hall's 571 is a separate matter entirely — it applies a different, later election (November 2024) that does not govern Jones's seat at all. A Deputy Registrar's email to Hall, dated May 16, 2025, shows Hall's own framework actually placed Jones in the 2022 bucket alongside Aaron Rawls, using the same 3,749-voter figure Workman used — only council members elected in 2024 (Lawson, Mei, Gravely) were meant to use the 5,708 figure under Hall's own stated interpretation. Why 571 nonetheless became publicly associated with this specific petition remains an open question, addressed further in the Notion research record.

Patti Covington — Beyond the Petition

Covington's role in this record does not end with the petition's outcome. She remained a documented public source on the underlying investigation afterward — it was Covington who publicly confirmed the Virginia State Police investigation remained active as of April 21, 2026, the same day Jones was reinstated to office. That confirmation is detailed in Chapter 7.
