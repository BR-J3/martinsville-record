Person Record · Full Career

Eric Payne

City Attorney Designee (2024) → Director of Development, EDA (2025–Present) → Key Witness, CL26-011

A Clinton White House aide turned whistleblower who won a $1.7 million jury verdict against the District of Columbia, recruited to Martinsville as City Attorney Designee — and then the man who personally saw both hot tubs, brought the office break-in to the Police Chief, and ended up subpoenaed in the very case his own hires were meant to prevent.

Washington, D.C. — Pre-2024 City Attorney Designee — 2024–2025 Witness / Director of Development — 2024–Present
Era One
Washington, D.C.
Pre-2024

Payne's career began as one of the youngest aides in the Clinton presidential administration, working in the White House Office of Communications Research. From there he built a 30-year career spanning the District of Columbia's Office of the Chief Financial Officer (where he served as Director of Contracts), Chief of Staff at the U.S. Department of Commerce's Economic Development Administration Bureau, a senior role at the Chicago Housing Authority, and international work managing multi-billion-dollar foreign military sales contracts in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia. He holds a law degree from the University of Arkansas and completed coursework toward a Master of Law in Government Procurement Law at George Washington University.

The Whistleblower Case — Adjudicated, Not Alleged

In 2009, while serving as a senior D.C. contracting official, Payne reported irregularities involving two city council members' undue influence over a $228 million lottery contract to two separate investigative agencies. He was demoted, then fired. He sued the District in 2010.

"The biggest victory is I have my name again, my integrity. I can start my life over again, and my family no longer has to suffer."
— Eric Payne, to the Washington Post, on the verdict

After a two-week federal trial, a jury found in 2016 that 10 of Payne's disclosures were legally protected whistleblowing, and awarded him $1.7 million in damages for wrongful demotion and reputational harm — six years after he first reported the contract irregularities. Payne has said he was effectively blackballed in Washington afterward and took an overseas position with the Air Force for three years before returning to the region.

What The Record Shows

This is the most concretely adjudicated whistleblower case connected to anyone in this entire record — an actual federal jury verdict with named damages, not a pending complaint or an unresolved allegation. It predates anything in Martinsville by nearly a decade and gives Payne's later role here a specific, documented credential: he had already been proven right once, at real personal cost, before Martinsville ever recruited him.

Era Two
City Attorney Designee
August 2024 – Summer 2025

The Martinsville City Council appointed Payne City Attorney Designee during a special called session on August 15, 2024, with plans for him to assume the full City Attorney title once admitted to the Virginia State Bar — he was already a member of the D.C. and Maryland bars. He was also named acting Managing Director of Development in the interim. He officially began the role September 1, 2024, at an annual salary of $185,000.

How He Actually Got There

Payne's own account, given in a retrospective interview roughly two years into his Martinsville tenure, confirms a detail long suspected but not previously documented on this page: he first came to Martinsville because a friend had been given the opportunity to develop a hotel on Commonwealth Boulevard, and asked Payne to attend a meeting in the city on his behalf as a senior advisor. Payne had never heard of Martinsville before that trip. Two weeks later, he was being interviewed by city officials for the City Attorney Designee role.

"If you want someone who's going to come to Martinsville, work hard each and every day on behalf of Martinsville, and call balls and strikes and be an honest guy, I'm your man. If you want someone who's going to come here and choose sides and be partisan, do not hire me."
— Eric Payne, on what he told council during his interview, August 2024
"It could not have worked out better for the City of Martinsville. We really need his experience; it's almost like fate how this worked out."
— Aaron Rawls, Vice Mayor, on Payne's appointment, August 2024

Mayor Jones and Councilwoman Kathy Lawson offered similarly enthusiastic public praise at the time, calling the hire proof the city could attract "top-tier talent."

What He Actually Did

As Interim Director of Development, Payne says he helped secure $1.5 million in opioid abatement authority grants for the city, brought in an outside firm to inspect commercial corridors for code violations, and coordinated Martinsville's role in opioid litigation against pharmacy benefit managers — litigation he says will make Martinsville the first locality in the county to go to trial, with Judge G. Carter Greer presiding, the same judge who later suspended Jones. Separately, Payne says his internal coordinating role helped Martinsville prevail in a Public Service Authority case worth more than $10 million, and helped get the city dismissed as a defendant in a wrongful-termination suit filed by a former Martinsville Assistant Commonwealth's Attorney.

Open Question

The wrongful-termination suit Payne references — filed by a former Assistant Commonwealth's Attorney — plausibly describes Mook v. Hall (documented on Hall's person-page), since the City of Martinsville is also a named defendant in that case alongside Hall personally. This record has not independently confirmed that Payne's account refers to the same case, and the established record on Hall's page shows the case still active against Hall as of mid-2025 — so if this is the same suit, only the city's portion of it would have been resolved, not Hall's. Flagged, not assumed.

The Bar Admission Delay — Two Accounts of the Same Transition

Payne's contract required Virginia Bar admission by April 1, 2025. Mayor Jones later said Payne faced "family medical issues" that delayed those plans. Rather than extend the contract or let it lapse, the city moved him into a newly created staff role with the city's Economic Development Authority, assuming the role July 1, 2025 — a position he had already been informally supporting during his time as City Attorney Designee.

"He gave up those positions to take the job under the EDA. He doesn't work for council or the city manager anymore. They have their own budget, and he'll operate under their purview."
— LC Jones, on Payne's transition, mid-2025

Payne's own retrospective account frames the move as a management decision: "She made the determination as the city manager that shifting to this EDA role may be more appropriate," he said of Ferrell-Benavides. A separate source gives a different characterization of the same period: the Brown Edwards hiring-practices audit states Payne continued to be paid at his $185,000 City Attorney Designee rate "until he was placed on paid administrative leave in June 2025" — a detail neither Payne's own account nor Jones's public statements mention. This record presents both characterizations rather than resolving them; they may describe a brief leave immediately preceding the July 1 EDA start date, or simply reflect a harsher audit characterization of the same underlying transition. Full detail on the audit itself: Chapter 5.

Era Three
Witness / Director of Development
2024 – Present

Payne is now the EDA's first economic development director, identifying redevelopment properties and pursuing grant opportunities. He is also one of the most direct fact-witnesses in the entire CL26-011 case — present, by his own account, at nearly every key moment of the hot tub timeline.

Open Question

Payne advised Jones informally on a legal-adjacent matter (the HCSO resignation) while serving as the city's own City Attorney Designee, at the same City Manager's direction. Whether that overlap was ever formally examined for conflict-of-interest purposes is not established in any source reviewed.

What The Record Shows

Payne occupies an unusual position in this record: recruited specifically for his credibility as someone who had already stood up to government corruption once, he ended up as a central witness in exactly the kind of conduct his own background should have made him least likely to overlook. Whether that is best read as the system working as intended — a person of demonstrated integrity surfacing what he saw — or as evidence of how normalized the underlying conduct had become even to someone with his specific history, isn't resolved here. Both readings are available from the same facts.
