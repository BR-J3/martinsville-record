Eric Phillips

Delegate, Virginia House District 48 — January 2024 to Present

A Henry County small business owner who won a state legislative seat in a special election, then won it again twice more — while also chairing Martinsville's own economic development authority. Not a central figure in the Jones/Ferrell-Benavides governance crisis, but a real structural presence in the regional economic-development story this record is watching unfold next door.

Era One
The Special Election
January 2024

Phillips, a Henry County native who owns and operates a small pickup-and-delivery trucking business, ran for the District 48 seat after Delegate Les Adams resigned December 12, 2023 to "make himself available for another position of service." District 48 covers parts of Henry and Pittsylvania counties and the entirety of the city of Martinsville.

The January 9, 2024 special election pitted Phillips against Democrat Melody Cartwright — a Martinsville native with over 25 years in public service as a graphic and exhibit designer for the Virginia Museum of Natural History. Phillips campaigned on agriculture, manufacturing, and higher education; Cartwright on women's rights, affordable housing, and education, citing her experience as a state employee rather than prior elected office.

"There's always a fight in any state, and particularly with our area, to get the money that we not only need but that we deserve. The taxpayers here pay taxes just like Northern Virginia, Richmond, and Hampton Roads."
— Eric Phillips, campaign statement, January 2024
January 9, 2024 — Special Election Results
Eric J. Phillips (R)69.6%
Melody A. Cartwright (D)30.3%

Phillips took office January 10, 2024. He has said the win felt "humbling," and that he wanted to "hopefully doing right by the citizens of the 48th District."

Era Two
The Legislative Record
2024 – 2025

Phillips and Cartwright faced each other again in the November 4, 2025 general election — this time a three-way race that also included independent Barbara Mayhew Hancock. Phillips won decisively a second time.

"I feel like it was good. It was a lot of hard work campaigning, but it's also a lot of hard work being a delegate. But I really enjoy it."
— Eric Phillips, on his 2025 re-election, November 2025

His current term runs through January 12, 2028. His stated legislative priorities have included regional infrastructure and energy grid reliability, support for the Blue Ridge Innovation Corridor as a vehicle for connecting Southside Virginia to broader economic growth, and closing what he's described as a persistent funding gap between rural Southside localities and Virginia's wealthier urban regions.

Phillips also serves as Western Vice Chair of the Republican Party of Virginia's State Executive Committee, and as a board member of Martinsville's Uptown Partnership.

Era Three
The EDA Chair
Ongoing

Separate from his legislative role, Phillips chairs the City of Martinsville's Industrial Development Authority — the body more commonly referred to locally as the city's Economic Development Authority. This is a city-level body, distinct from the Henry County Industrial Development Authority, which is the entity that actually conducted the May 2026 sale of the Bryant Property ("Patriot Centre II") to Appalachian Power.

Open Question — Precision Matters Here

No source reviewed places Phillips in any direct role in the Patriot Centre II transaction itself — none of the officials quoted in coverage of that sale (Henry County Administrator Dale Wagoner, IDA Chairman Len Dillon, Supervisors Chairman Jim Adams, or Appalachian Power's Brian Abraham) mention him. The connection that does exist is structural, not transactional: Phillips simultaneously represents the district in the General Assembly, chairs a separate but related city economic-development body, and has publicly championed the Blue Ridge Innovation Corridor — the same regional development push the Patriot Centre II sale could plausibly feed into. That overlap is worth watching. It is not, as of this writing, evidence of any specific involvement in that specific deal.

What The Record Shows

Phillips is not a figure in the Jones/Ferrell-Benavides governance crisis that is the core of this record. He's documented here because his roles — state delegate for the district, chair of a city economic-development authority, and a public advocate for the same regional development corridor implicated in the AEP land deal — sit at exactly the kind of overlapping-office intersection this record tracks carefully elsewhere. The overlap is real and worth recording. Anything beyond that is speculation this record isn't willing to make.
