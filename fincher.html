Robert Fincher

Police Officer (1994) → Chief of Police (2023) → Interim City Manager (2025) → City Manager (2026–Present)

Thirty-one years with the Martinsville Police Department before stepping into city government. Three documented eras, three distinct accountability questions. This page pulls together everything on the public record about one person, across his full career — not just the chapter where he happens to appear.

Era One
Chief of Police
January 2023 – August 2025 · Final 3 of 31 years with Martinsville PD

Fincher rose through the Martinsville Police Department over three decades — officer (1994), sergeant, captain, deputy chief, and finally Chief of Police beginning January 2023. Two documented incidents from this era raise accountability questions that remain open in the public record.

Two Separate Incidents, One Public Dispute

Charles Roark, a Star News TV reporter, was involved in two distinct physical incidents while covering local news during Fincher's tenure as Police Chief — three years apart, involving different officers. Both became part of a single public dispute over the department's handling of them.

Incident One — 2023, Officer Harper, Northview. Roark's own camera, recording footage at the time, captured two separate confrontations with bystanders who objected to being filmed. In the first, an older male resident intervened on Roark's behalf, telling the other party to leave him alone. In the second, a different bystander knocked Roark's phone away during a physical confrontation; another bystander intervened and warned the aggressor she could get in trouble. The footage ends with Officer Harper picking up the phone. Roark says Capt. Wayne Davis later admitted Harper had given the phone to a woman nearby — not secured, not returned to Roark directly, and the phone was ultimately discarded in a city trash bin.

Incident Two — 2024, Officer Harmon and Officer Kanode, Tequila's. Roark followed a police arrest in progress to the cruiser outside Tequila's Sports Bar; Officer Harmon knocked the phone from Roark's hand. Separately, Roark was physically assaulted by Andrea Branton on the public sidewalk outside the same venue, left bloodied. The venue's manager wrongly blamed Roark for the altercation and trespassed him from the property, despite Roark's denial — while bystanders in the crowd mocked him. Officer Samuel Kanode responded to the assault.

More than a year after Incident One, in June 2024, the department still had not released information. Mayor LC Jones, on the record to the Martinsville Bulletin, did not hold back: "The Chief is just stepping back, washing his hands and hoping it goes away!" He went further: "It looks like a good-ole system."

"It is classified as a personnel case."
— Robert Fincher, Police Chief, to the Martinsville Bulletin, June 2024

Fincher's June 2024 statement named Officer Kanode specifically as having failed to seek medical attention for Roark or file a report following the Tequila's assault. Separately, in an earlier May 2024 statement, Fincher publicly defended Officer Harmon over the cruiser/camera incident, telling BTW: "Officers are human too."

The Office Break-In, and the Erased Computer

In early February 2025, City Manager Aretha Ferrell-Benavides's office was broken into and her computer rummaged through. Eric Payne discovered law enforcement had not been informed, and personally contacted Fincher to start a criminal investigation, which came to include a Virginia State Police investigator.

"Unfortunately, when the investigator arrived, a member of the city manager's staff had erased the computer and all forensic data that would have supported or debunked a computer breach."
— Eric Payne, reported by the Martinsville Bulletin, August 2025

Mayor Jones called for a review of building security camera footage and data key card logs to determine who had entered and departed, and when. Payne said he was unclear what, if any, investigative action followed.

Open Question

Who was the city manager's staff member who erased the computer? Was this person ever identified, questioned, or disciplined? Did the camera/key-card review Jones requested ever happen? None of these are answered in the public record found so far.

What The Record Shows — Era One

Twice during Fincher's tenure as Police Chief, his department's handling of an incident involving the city's own communications or records became a matter of public dispute. In one case, the mayor himself called the department's silence "a good-ole system." In the other, the central piece of evidence was destroyed by a city employee after a state investigator had already arrived. Neither matter has a public resolution on record.

Era Two
Interim City Manager
August 2025 – March 2026

Following Ferrell-Benavides's termination, the council appointed Fincher Interim City Manager. He had not contemplated a job in city management.

"Last month my career took an unexpected turn. I was asked by the City Council to serve as the Interim City Manager. After 31 years in law enforcement I was looking at retiring and possibly starting a new career. I had never contemplated a job in city management, but God will sometimes open doors that you least expect. Taking the job (although it is temporary) was not an easy decision as there are many changes that need to be made and restoring the public's trust in government is not an easy task."
— Robert Fincher, LinkedIn, September 2025

It was during this period that Fincher discovered the March 18, 2025 offer letter — bearing Jones's sole signature — sitting in Ferrell-Benavides's city email draft folder. That discovery became central to the Sands Anderson investigation and the case against Jones. (Full detail: Chapter 4.)

Ordinary city business continued alongside the turmoil. Fincher, Vice Mayor Kathy Lawson, and Councilman Julian Mei toured the nearly-complete Aaron Mills Apartments — 52 units of affordable senior housing on Aaron Street, developed by Landmark Development, with rents from $542 to $800 monthly.

What The Record Shows — Era Two

Fincher's own words describe taking the interim role as unexpected and difficult, with "restoring the public's trust" already identified by him as the central task — months before the cyberattack and before his own permanent appointment made that task his directly, not just an inherited problem.

Era Three
City Manager
March 11, 2026 – Present

The Martinsville City Council formally appointed Fincher permanent City Manager on March 11, 2026, ending his interim status. Vice Mayor Kathy Lawson: "Rob's leadership during the interim period has exceeded all expectations."

Two weeks later, the city experienced its most serious documented crisis of his tenure.

The Cyberattack, and the Walk-Back

March 25, 2026 — the city announces a cyberattack, contained the same day. Officials state plainly: "no consumer or citizen information has been compromised."

April 7, 2026 — nearly two weeks later, systems remain down. Fincher, to the Bulletin: "I hate being so short and not giving you much information, but due to the active investigation, we cannot share any details at this time."

April 9, 2026 — city says services are "fully operational again." The "no information compromised" claim is repeated.

Later — the city's own website posts a formal "Notice of Cyber Incident," offering free identity monitoring, credit freeze guidance, and fraud alert instructions, with a dedicated assistance line active through August 31, 2026.

What The Record Shows — The Contradiction

A city does not offer free credit monitoring, credit freeze guidance, and fraud alert instructions for an incident where "no consumer or citizen information was compromised." Those are the standard remedies for a confirmed exposure of personal information. The city's early public statements and its own later formal notice are not reconcilable as written.

Open Question

Whose job was cybersecurity at the City of Martinsville? No named IT security officer or contracted security vendor has been identified in the public record so far. Worth checking against existing bank statement research (recurring IT-adjacent vendor charges already documented elsewhere in this hub) to see if any named vendor maps to security specifically.

What The Record Shows — Era Three, Overall

Fincher's permanent tenure was two weeks old when the most significant documented crisis of his career as City Manager began. The city's public messaging about that crisis shifted from outright denial of any data compromise to, eventually, a formal notice offering breach-specific remedies — the same basic pattern of public statement followed by quiet contradiction that recurs throughout this record.
