The Legal Chain

Special Prosecutors · Attorney General · Suspension · Reinstatement

A citizen's petition, a sitting prosecutor recused by a judge who then retired mid-case, an Attorney General who declined to step in over a technicality, and two special prosecutors after him before the third one dismissed the case. The removal petition against Mayor LC Jones moved through five different officials before it ever reached a jury.

The Record

Virginia Code 24.2-235 governs the removal of local elected officials from office. When a citizen's petition is filed, the sitting Commonwealth's Attorney is legally required to review it — and may ask the court to appoint a special prosecutor if a conflict exists. In Commonwealth v. Jones, Case CL26-011, that single question — who would actually handle the case — took four months, five officials, and a request to the Virginia Attorney General before the case ever reached a substantive hearing.

The case alleged violations of the Virginia Conflict of Interests Act and the bribery statute against Mayor LC Jones, based on the four undisclosed benefits documented in Chapter 2. It ended on May 15, 2026, dismissed with prejudice — not on the merits of those allegations, but on a signature-threshold question, detailed in Chapter 6 and Chapter 8.

The Petition and the First Recusal Fight

On January 13, 2026, Martinsville resident Patti Covington filed a citizen's petition seeking Jones's removal, ultimately certified with 401 valid signatures. Patrick Flinn — who had just taken office as Commonwealth's Attorney, succeeding Andy Hall — was legally required to review it. His first act was to ask then-Circuit Judge G. Carter Greer to appoint a special prosecutor in his place, citing his own professional and personal relationships with city council members, staff, and potential witnesses. Greer denied the motion on January 15, 2026.

With no special prosecutor appointed, Flinn proceeded to review the petition himself. On February 17, 2026, he filed formal notice that valid grounds existed for the case to move forward. The next day, Greer ordered a recall trial and suspended Jones pending its outcome.

Andy Hall's Separate, Earlier Connection

Andy Hall, Flinn's predecessor, was never part of the CL26-011 prosecutor chain — his term had already ended by the time the petition was filed. Hall's connection to this case is earlier and separate: in September 2025, while still in office, he referred the criminal investigation into city spending under Ferrell-Benavides to Bedford County Commonwealth's Attorney Wes Nance, citing his own appearance of a conflict. Nance's investigation and the civil removal petition were not formally combined until Bethany Harrison was appointed to both in April 2026 — at which point Flinn described them as "combined at the hip." Full detail on Hall's role is in his person-page.

A Judge Retires Mid-Case

Greer retired shortly after suspending Jones. The Virginia General Assembly appointed Stephanie Vipperman to his seat on the 21st Judicial Circuit, with her term beginning May 1, 2026. From this point forward, a rotation of substitute judges — including James McGarry and Marcus Brinks — handled the case's remaining proceedings.

The Attorney General — Declined Over a Technicality

By March 2026, Nance had been elevated to a circuit court judgeship, leaving the criminal investigation without a prosecutor at the same time Flinn was still seeking relief from the civil case. On March 13, 2026, Flinn formally requested that Attorney General Jay Jones be appointed special prosecutor for both the criminal and civil matters, and separately asked Governor Abigail Spanberger's office to facilitate the request. Flinn told reporters his message to the Governor's office was never returned.

On March 20, 2026, the Attorney General's office declined. According to a spokesperson, the office considered Flinn's request procedurally deficient — he had sent an email rather than submitting the formal request required to go through the Governor's office. The decision was not framed as a ruling on the merits of the case.

"The Attorney General has formally declined to assist the City of Martinsville. As I explained in my previous press release, in which I requested the Attorney General step in, the Code of Virginia effectively limits criminal investigations of elected officials and political subdivisions to the Attorney General, the Governor, or..."
— Patrick Flinn, public statement, March 20, 2026

Separately, Jones himself had also pushed for outside review — his attorneys filed pre-trial motions which Jones certified were copied to the Attorney General's office, and at one point asked the court to order the Attorney General to appear. Jones had publicly stated more than once that he wanted an outside agency to review the city's matters. Both the prosecutor seeking to recuse himself and the defendant facing removal wanted the Attorney General involved — for different reasons.

Harrison — Appointed to Both Cases, Then Withdraws

With the Attorney General's office unwilling to step in, Flinn renewed his motion to be relieved from the civil case. On April 1, 2026, substitute Judge James McGarry granted it, appointing Lynchburg Commonwealth's Attorney Bethany Harrison as special prosecutor — covering both the civil removal petition and Nance's former role overseeing the criminal investigation into the audit.

On April 21, 2026, substitute Judge Marcus Brinks reinstated Jones to the city council pending trial. Harrison, appearing in the case for the only time since her appointment, offered no objection to the reinstatement — but told the court of a possible conflict that might prevent her from continuing.

One week later, on April 28, 2026, Harrison moved to withdraw, stating she had done so "due to a material conflict and after consulting with the Virginia State Bar Ethics Hotline." She declined to specify the conflict. The court appointed Alfred Gray Collins III, Commonwealth's Attorney for Colonial Heights, as her replacement that same day — covering both the civil petition and the criminal investigation, as Harrison had.

  • Appeared once in the case — the April 21 hearing reinstating Jones — and raised no objection to reinstatement

What The Record Shows

Five officials touched this case before it reached a hearing on the merits: Greer (the judge who suspended Jones and then retired), Flinn (recused, twice tried, once denied), the Attorney General's office (declined on a technicality), Harrison (appointed, then withdrew over an undisclosed conflict), and Collins III (the prosecutor of record at dismissal). Two of those five — Greer's retirement and Harrison's conflict — were never publicly explained in detail. The case was ultimately decided on a question that had nothing to do with any of this: whether the petition had enough valid signatures.

The Suspension and Reinstatement

CH7-SUSPENSION Jones Suspended, Then Reinstated Feb 18 – Apr 21, 2026
  • February 18, 2026 — Judge Greer suspends Jones from mayoral duties, orders recall trial, statutory basis Va. Code 24.2-235
  • Jones challenged the suspension as unconstitutional, arguing the statute requires a speedy trial that took priority over other proceedings
  • April 21, 2026 — substitute Judge Marcus Brinks reinstates Jones to the city council pending trial
  • Patti Covington — the same Martinsville resident who filed the original petition (Chapter 6) — publicly confirmed the same day that the Virginia State Police investigation remained active

What Parties Refused to Do

Flinn noted in his case review that Jones and Ferrell-Benavides refused to cooperate with the investigation, writing that he was "frustrated by the refusal of parties involved to cooperate" and that many of those involved "have 'lawyered up.'" He proceeded on willing witnesses only.

The subpoena list for CL26-011 includes: LC Jones, Nikima Robinson, Eric Payne, Karen Roberts, Richard Stanfield, Peyton Nibblett, Robert Fincher, Kathy Lawson, Julian Mei, Rayshaun Gravely, and Aaron Rawls.

What The Record Shows

The two subjects of the removal petition refused to cooperate with the investigation. The prosecutor proceeded on willing witnesses only, while cautioning that anyone implicated is presumed innocent and that a separate special prosecutor had already been appointed to handle any criminal investigation specifically. Eleven individuals were subpoenaed to testify. The case was dismissed with prejudice before a jury heard any of it.

The VSP Investigation

Separate from CL26-011, the Virginia State Police opened a criminal investigation into Martinsville governance matters, growing out of the same spending allegations against Ferrell-Benavides. The dismissal of CL26-011 on May 15, 2026 did not close the VSP investigation. No charges have been announced. The investigation is ongoing as of this publication.

VSP Investigation — Active

The Virginia State Police investigation remains active as of April 21, 2026 — the last confirmed public documentation, via Patti Covington's statement. The dismissal of CL26-011 did not close the VSP investigation. These are separate proceedings. No charges have been announced.
