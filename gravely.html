Rayshaun Gravely

Martinsville City Council Member — 2022–Present · Term Expires 2028

A felony case that ended in a full jury acquittal, not the quiet plea deal an earlier version of this page described. A campaign where his own legal history became the subject of a council member's pointed remark about race — independently sourced nearly two years before that same remark became part of a federal lawsuit. This page corrects what needed correcting and documents the rest precisely.

Era One
The Case
2021 – 2023
Correction to an Earlier Version of This Page

An earlier version of this page described Gravely's case as a felony charge amended to a misdemeanor disorderly conduct conviction, citing a specific case number (GC22001064-00) with a nolo contendere plea and a fine paid in February 2025. That account does not match contemporaneous press reporting and has not been independently verified against an actual court docket. Multiple news outlets — and Commonwealth's Attorney Andy Hall himself — confirm a different outcome: a full jury trial and acquittal on all charges. This page now reflects that confirmed outcome. The earlier framing is retracted, not repeated.

The Incident and the Charges

On June 6, 2021, at a birthday party at the Sportsman's Club in Martinsville, a woman — also a Henry County Sheriff's Office deputy, and Gravely's on-and-off girlfriend — alleged that Gravely became angry after seeing her talking to another man, grabbed her arm and twisted it behind her back, pushed her against his car, and choked her. She did not report it at the time.

Nearly a year later, on April 16, 2022, Gravely was arrested in Charlotte, North Carolina on a separate allegation of domestic assault against the same woman. During that investigation, she disclosed the 2021 Martinsville incident. She sought a protective order in Virginia; a magistrate directed her instead to file a police report, which led to Virginia charges. Gravely turned himself in days later and was charged with strangulation, abduction by force or intimidation, assault and battery of a family member, and malicious wounding.

The Henry County Sheriff's Office fired Gravely within days of the Virginia charges being filed.

"In our administration of the Henry County Sheriff's Office, we have always expressed to every employee our expectation to conduct themselves as an example of actions and trust to our community. We have never tolerated any wrongful conduct if this expectation has not been upheld. In this, Rayshaun Gravely is no longer employed with the office."
— Henry County Sheriff's Office, public statement, April 2022

Bond and the Community That Showed Up

Gravely was held without bond initially, then granted $50,000 bond, later reduced to $25,000 at an appeal hearing before Circuit Court Judge G. Carter Greer. The courtroom drew roughly 30 spectators, including local pastors. Three people spoke on his behalf, offering to serve as custodians under the court's conditions: Sheila Turner of Spencer, who said she'd been like a "second mother" to Gravely since he was 14, and Lenny Noel of Rich Acres, who knew him from Martinsville Middle School. Bond conditions included a 7 p.m.–7 a.m. curfew, a prohibition on leaving Virginia except for supervised North Carolina court dates, required residence under his mother's supervision, mandatory employment, no alcohol or firearms, and continued GPS monitoring.

The Verdict

On October 10, 2023, a Martinsville Circuit Court jury took approximately 35 minutes to find Gravely not guilty on all four Virginia charges — assault and battery, strangulation with injury, malicious wounding, and abduction by force or intimidation. Commonwealth's Attorney Andy Hall confirmed the verdict directly to press at the time.

Open Question

The separate North Carolina case — a felony fugitive-extradition charge and a misdemeanor assault-on-a-female charge in Mecklenburg County — does not have a confirmed outcome in any source reviewed. It should not be assumed dropped, dismissed, or resolved either way until that's actually established.

What The Record Shows

Andy Hall's office did not just hold jurisdiction during this case in some passive sense — Hall personally confirmed the acquittal to the press. This is a direct, named connection between Hall and Gravely, three years before either man's name appeared in the petition-threshold dispute documented in Chapter 6.

Era Two
The Campaign
2024

Gravely ran for one of three open council seats in the November 2024 election. He was open about his legal history during the campaign. The Henry County Enterprise's August 23, 2024 coverage of the race — "Differing opinions create tension among leaders" — is also the original, contemporaneous source for a quote that would resurface nearly two years later in a federal lawsuit.

Jones Becomes a Mentor

Jones told the Enterprise he was aware of Gravely's prior charges and described them, in his own words, as "dropped" — a looser characterization than the actual record, which shows a full jury trial and acquittal, not a dismissal.

"I think what really describes his character is how he went through those things, and it made him a better person and more ambitious to help other individuals."
— LC Jones, on Gravely, Henry County Enterprise, August 2024

Jones described becoming a "mentor" to Gravely in the political field, tracing it to a specific conversation with Rawls and then-City Manager Ferrell-Benavides while preparing candidate packets.

The Exchange — Original Source for a 2026 Lawsuit Claim

Per Jones's account in the same article: when the conversation turned to Gravely, Rawls said he wouldn't support him because of his legal history.

The "White Vote" Exchange — Both Accounts, From 2024

Jones's account: Rawls told him he "wouldn't support him, and he was going to make sure that he wouldn't get a white vote, and nobody in the white community would support him."

Rawls's response, same article, same date: Jones "tried to make it a race thing to cover up his agenda because he can't support it with substance." Rawls's stated objection was that Gravely has "zero translatable skills or experience to this job," adding, "LC knows better than anyone that this is not a job for amateurs at this point in time. We need real people to bring real solutions."

Rawls separately said: "When you have somebody like that in charge, because they don't know what they're doing, then taxpayers start to see their money disappearing really fast." He also said Gravely "has been arrested three times for hurting women."

Open Question

Only two arrests tied to domestic-violence allegations are independently confirmed anywhere in this record — the April 2022 Virginia charges and the separate April 2022 North Carolina charge, both stemming from the same underlying relationship. A third arrest is not established in any source reviewed. Rawls's "three times" is presented here as his own characterization, not a confirmed count.

What The Record Shows

This same exchange — Jones's account of Rawls's remark, and Rawls's denial of the racial framing — resurfaces nearly word-for-word in Jones's June 9, 2026 federal lawsuit against Rawls (documented in Chapter 9), filed almost two years later. That doesn't resolve which characterization is accurate. It does mean the claim isn't solely a product of 2026 litigation — both men were on the record with their respective positions in real time, in August 2024, well before any lawsuit existed.

Era Three
On Council
2025 – Present

The NAACP Funding Vote — November 5, 2025

Council deadlocked 2-2 on a motion to fund the local NAACP chapter's community outreach programs. Gravely voted for the funding, alongside Mayor Jones. Rawls and Mei voted against. Vice Mayor Kathy Lawson was absent, which created the deadlock.

CH6-M1 NAACP Funding Vote — Aftermath Nov 5, 2025
  • Motion failed 2-2: Jones and Gravely for; Rawls and Mei against; Lawson absent
  • Despite voting against city funding, Rawls and Mei each personally pledged $500 to the NAACP
  • Jones personally pledged $500 as well
  • Combined $1,500 in personal donations followed the failed public funding vote
Source: Star News TV, November 5, 2025, confirmed in hand
What The Record Shows

This is the same Jones/Gravely versus Rawls/Mei factional split visible throughout the broader governance crisis — but the aftermath cuts against a simple villain narrative. All three council members present found their own way to support the same cause personally, regardless of how they voted publicly.

The Affidavit — A Different Role Than Jones's

Rawls's sworn affidavit places Gravely in two specific moments. On May 13, 2025, when Jones approached Rawls aggressively during a public meeting, Gravely physically intercepted Jones before police arrived. On November 12, 2025, Gravely was one of four council members present for the closed session on the forensic audit's preliminary findings. Neither moment attributes disputed conduct to Gravely himself — in the May 13 incident specifically, the affidavit describes him acting to de-escalate, not provoke.

Open Question

Court records show inconsistent name spellings across filings ("Rayshaun," "Rayshawn," "O'Shea," "Oshea," "Junio," "Jumon"). Whether this tracks a change between his sheriff's-deputy-era records and his council-era records has not been independently confirmed.
