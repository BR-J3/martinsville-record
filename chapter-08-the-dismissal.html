The Dismissal

The Record

Commonwealth v. Jones, Case CL26-011, was dismissed with prejudice on May 15, 2026. A dismissal with prejudice means the case cannot be refiled on the same grounds. The prosecution is over. Jones cannot be retried on these charges.

What the dismissal did not do: it did not release the Brown Edwards forensic audit. It did not close the Virginia State Police investigation. It did not resolve the five active federal lawsuits. It did not return the $40,000 in taxpayer funds spent on the prosecution. It did not change the underlying facts documented in Chapters 1 through 7.

Jones publicly called it total vindication. The record documents what existed the day after.

What the Dismissal Means — And What It Does Not

A dismissal with prejudice in a civil removal proceeding means the specific petition — with its specific charges, filed by specific prosecutors — cannot be pursued further. It is a final disposition of CL26-011 as a legal matter.

It does not mean the underlying facts were found to be false. It does not mean a court heard the evidence and acquitted. It means the case ended before a jury considered the evidence. The documented record — the offer letter, the airfare, the hot tub, the employment attempt, the legal arrangement, the undisclosed Statement of Economic Interests — remains in the public record regardless of the dismissal.

The dismissal also did not resolve two separate claims raised around Ferrell-Benavides's own termination, documented in Chapter 4: her public statement that racial tension played a factor in her dismissal, and Councilman Rayshaun Gravely's statement that the investigation's scope extended beyond the city manager alone. CL26-011 concerned Jones, not Ferrell-Benavides's termination directly — but both claims remain open and unresolved in the public record, the same as the audit and the VSP investigation.

What The Record Shows

CL26-011 was dismissed with prejudice on May 15, 2026. No jury heard the evidence. No court ruled on the underlying facts. The forensic audit remained sealed. The VSP investigation remained active. The dismissal ended one proceeding. It did not end the record.

The $40,000 — Taxpayer Cost of the Prosecution

The prosecution of CL26-011 cost Martinsville taxpayers approximately $40,000. That figure covers the cost of the special prosecutor chain — Nance, Harrison, and Collins III — and associated prosecution expenses through dismissal.

This is separate from the cost of the Brown Edwards forensic audit, which cost the city over $500,000. It is separate from the legal fees the city has incurred defending the five active federal lawsuits. The $40,000 is the prosecution cost alone — the cost of the removal proceeding that ended on May 15.

What Remained Sealed at Dismissal

When CL26-011 was dismissed on May 15, 2026, the Brown Edwards forensic audit had not been publicly released. The city had acknowledged the audit's existence. A FOIA request had confirmed 1.6 gigabytes of records. The public had received a summary. The full report was withheld under the active litigation exemption — Virginia Code 2.2-3705.1.

With the dismissal of CL26-011, one of the litigation exemptions ended. However, the five active federal lawsuits filed in the months surrounding the dismissal — including two filed June 9, 2026 — provide continued grounds for withholding. The full audit release is expected July 14–24, 2026. Chapter 5 publishes when it drops.

Audit Still Sealed — As Of This Publication

The Brown Edwards forensic audit remains sealed as of this publication. 1.6 gigabytes of records confirmed by city FOIA response. Full release expected July 14–24, 2026. Chapter 5 of this record publishes on that date. The $10M figure documented in the city's own graphic represents findings from that audit. The full findings are not yet public.

Twenty-Five Days Later

On June 9, 2026 — twenty-five days after the dismissal of CL26-011 — two new federal lawsuits were filed in the Western District of Virginia, Roanoke Division:

Jones v. Rawls and Sands Anderson — Mayor Jones suing the council member who filed the federal complaint and the law firm that conducted the investigation.

Ferrell-Benavides v. City of Martinsville, Rawls, and Sands Anderson — The fired city manager suing the city, the council member, and the investigating law firm. Seeking $220,000 or more in unpaid severance plus additional claims.

Both docket numbers were pending PACER indexing as of June 11, 2026. Chapter 9 covers all five active federal cases.
