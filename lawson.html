Kathy Lawson

Vice Mayor, City of Martinsville — Term Expires 2028

Of everyone documented in this record, Lawson is the one most often defined by what wasn't said or wasn't there — an absence that broke a tied vote, a compensation question she chose not to answer in specifics, and a quote about her own credibility that traces back through two other people before it ever reaches her. This page documents exactly what is, and isn't, established.

Era One
The Vice Mayor
2025 – Present

Lawson holds one of three council seats — alongside Julian Mei and Rayshaun Gravely — with terms running through 2028, placing her outside the November 2026 election cycle entirely. Most of what's documented about her in this record is ordinary, procedural council business, set against the backdrop of the broader governance crisis rather than at its center.

During Robert Fincher's interim period as City Manager, Lawson joined him and Councilman Mei on a tour of the nearly-complete Aaron Mills Apartments — a 52-unit affordable senior housing development on Aaron Street. Logged here simply as evidence that routine city business continued through the crisis period, not as anything notable on its own.

When council formally appointed Fincher permanent City Manager in March 2026, Lawson offered a supportive, on-record quote.

"Rob's leadership during the interim period has exceeded all expectations."
— Kathy Lawson, on Robert Fincher's permanent appointment, March 2026

For petition-threshold purposes, Lawson falls into the same calculation bucket as Mei and Gravely under Andy Hall's own May 2025 framework — council members elected in 2024 use that year's 5,708-voter turnout figure. This distinction never mattered in practice, since the petition concerned Jones's seat specifically, governed by the earlier 2022 election instead.

Era Two
The Absence
November 2025

On November 5, 2025, City Council took up a motion to fund the local NAACP chapter's community outreach and civic engagement programs. The vote deadlocked 2-2 — Jones and Gravely in favor, Rawls and Mei against — and failed for lack of a majority. Lawson was absent. Her absence is what produced the tie; with all five members present, the motion would have passed or failed outright rather than deadlocking.

Open Question

No public explanation for the absence has surfaced in any source reviewed. An absence on its own — illness, a scheduling conflict, any number of ordinary reasons — shouldn't be read as deliberate or political without further evidence. It's documented here because of its effect on the outcome, not because a motive has been established.

Separately, in a Star News TV interview, Charles Roark asked Lawson about Robert Fincher's compensation under his new permanent City Manager contract. Lawson declined to discuss specific figures, though she indicated the contract includes a clause allowing Fincher to remain in the role indefinitely, provided he gives advance notice before leaving. Aaron Rawls, asked a comparable question separately, answered more directly.

Open Question

Declining to discuss a colleague's specific compensation figures in a press interview is not unusual practice for an elected official, and shouldn't automatically be read as evasiveness. The contrast with Rawls's more direct answer on the same general topic is worth noting as documented, but the exact contract language has not been independently confirmed, and no inference beyond that contrast is drawn here.

Era Three
The Disputed Quote
May 2026

At the May 15, 2026 hearing that ultimately dismissed CL26-011, Jones's attorney Mark Krudys argued in open court that he'd received an email from special prosecutor Collins referencing an earlier statement Aaron Rawls had reportedly made about Lawson.

"You'd have a better chance of getting a confession out of Jones than the truth out of Lawson."
— Attributed by Krudys, in open court, to a statement by Aaron Rawls referenced in an email from special prosecutor Collins
Open Question

This quote reaches the public record through three layers of attribution — Krudys's in-court characterization, of an email from Collins, describing a statement attributed to Rawls. Lawson has made no public response to it. It is documented here because it is now part of the official court record of the May 15 hearing, not because it has been independently verified as something Rawls actually said, or because it reflects anything established about Lawson's honesty.

What The Record Shows

Krudys raised this specific statement to argue that Rawls was one of two people — alongside Eric Payne — with a personal stake in the outcome, not as a freestanding claim about Lawson's character. Its purpose in the courtroom was to undercut Rawls's credibility as a source for the case against Jones. Read in that context, it says more about the dispute between Krudys and Rawls than it does about Lawson, who was not present to respond to it and has not commented on it since.
