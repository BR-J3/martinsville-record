Northview Gardens

755 Stultz Road — A Martinsville Address That Isn't Martinsville's Jurisdiction

A 100-unit federally subsidized apartment complex with a Martinsville mailing address that actually sits in Henry County — already documented elsewhere in this record for a 2023 incident involving a journalist's confiscated phone. This page covers the property itself: a 2023 fatal shooting, a HUD contract running through 2034, and a March 2026 sale to an undisclosed buyer that one county supervisor confirmed publicly, once, and never followed up on.

Part One
The Property
Built 1976

Northview Gardens — also referred to in some records as Northview Garden Apartments — sits at 755 Stultz Road, carrying a Martinsville mailing address despite falling under Henry County jurisdiction, the same jurisdictional split already documented on Robert Fincher's and Wayne Davis's pages in connection with a separate 2023 incident at the same property. Built in 1976, the complex spans 8.74 acres across ten two-story apartment buildings plus a leasing office, with a unit mix of 21 one-bedroom, 49 two-bedroom, and 30 three-bedroom apartments — 100 units total.

NORTHVIEW-HUD Federal Housing Profile Current
  • HUD Project ID 800021980
  • HAP (Housing Assistance Payment) contract covers 20% of units, running through September 2034
  • REAC physical-condition score declined from 91 (2016) to 83b (2019) — the most recent score identified in materials reviewed
  • Located in a 2023 Qualified Census Tract, carrying a 30% basis boost for federal Low-Income Housing Tax Credit financing
  • Marketed to investors by SAB Capital prior to the 2026 sale, explicitly highlighting the HAP contract and LIHTC/QCT status as selling points
  • Managed day-to-day by Vista Capital Management (Greenville, SC)
Source: SAB Capital listing materials, HUD project records, confirmed in hand
What The Record Shows

This is federally subsidized housing, marketed to outside investors in part because of the federal subsidy itself, sitting in a jurisdiction split that already produced one documented accountability gap elsewhere in this record. Both facts matter independently of each other and are worth holding together.

Part Two
The Shooting
February 14, 2023

On the night of February 14, 2023, the Henry County 911 Communications Center received a call reporting a gunshot victim at Northview Gardens. Henry County Sheriff's deputies found Deshawn Marquis Ross, 22, of Bassett, inside an apartment with a gunshot wound; Henry County Public Safety EMS began treatment, but Ross died at the scene.

Investigators determined an argument had occurred between Ross and Jahari Keontae Pritchett, 22, of Martinsville, before Pritchett produced a firearm, shot Ross, and fled. Pritchett was taken into custody without incident the next morning in the Axton area of the county and charged with first-degree murder, use of a firearm in the commission of a felony, and possession of a firearm by a convicted felon.

"I always said I never wanted to be that mother who got that phone call saying your kid just got shot."
— Star Ross, Deshawn Ross's mother, to WFXR, March 2, 2023

Ross's mother told WFXR that gun violence and crime had "gotten out of hand" in the Martinsville area, and asked that her son be remembered as a good son and father. Pritchett's case proceeded through the courts over the following two years; he was ultimately sentenced to 30 years in prison for second-degree murder in connection with the killing — a reduced charge from the original first-degree murder count.

NORTHVIEW-SHOOTING The Ross/Pritchett Case — Key Facts Feb 2023 – Sentencing
  • Victim: Deshawn Marquis Ross, 22, of Bassett — died at the scene
  • Charged: Jahari Keontae Pritchett, 22, of Martinsville — first-degree murder, firearm charges
  • Henry County Sheriff's Office jurisdiction, not Martinsville Police, consistent with the property's actual location
  • Outcome: sentenced to 30 years for second-degree murder, a reduced charge from the original first-degree count
Source: Martinsville Bulletin, WDBJ7, BTW21, WFXR — confirmed in hand
A Separate 2023 Incident, Same Property

This shooting is not the same event as the 2023 Northview incident already documented on Fincher's, Roark's, and Davis's pages — that incident involved Charles Roark's confiscated phone during a Henry County Sheriff's Office response, and is a separate, unrelated matter that happens to have occurred at the same complex in the same year. The two should not be merged into a single "Northview incident."

Part Three
The Sale
Closed March 2026

The sale of Northview Gardens closed in March 2026, brokered by The Kirkland Company (Brentwood, TN), with Virginia-licensed brokers Braeden O'Brien and Austin Haney handling the transaction. The buyer has not been publicly identified. As of this writing, Henry County's own GIS property records still list Rigamaz Northview LLC as the owner — likely because the deed transfer has not yet been formally recorded, not because the sale didn't happen.

Henry County Supervisor Chris Lawless confirmed the sale directly and publicly — responding, while on vacation, to a comment on a Star News TV post. Lawless confirmed the property had sold, that renovation was underway, and that few or no tenants remained on site.

NORTHVIEW-LAWLESS Supervisor Lawless's Public Comment Reply 2026
  • Confirmed: property sold, renovation underway, few or no tenants remained at the time of his reply
  • Responded from what he described as his own perspective, framed around his role as a parent
  • This is the only public engagement on record from Lawless on this specific matter; a planned follow-up inquiry was never answered
Source: Star News TV public comment thread, confirmed in hand
Open Questions — Genuinely Unresolved

Who is the actual buyer? Was HUD properly notified of the ownership transfer, as required for a property carrying an active HAP contract through 2034? What happened to the roughly 20 HUD-assisted households Lawless said were largely gone by the time of his reply — relocated with assistance, or simply displaced? None of these are answered in any source reviewed.

What The Record Shows

A federally subsidized apartment complex with an active 20-year HUD contract changed hands through an out-of-state broker to an undisclosed buyer, confirmed publicly by exactly one elected official, exactly once, in an informal social media reply rather than any formal public notice. Whatever happened to the households living there during that transition has not been documented anywhere in the public record found so far.
