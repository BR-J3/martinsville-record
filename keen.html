Reva Keen

Martinsville City Sheriff's Office — Assigned to City Council Security

For months, this page documented Keen's role entirely through Rawls's complaint, since she had never given her own account. She has since filed a sworn declaration in federal court — and it doesn't just add her side, it directly contradicts Rawls's complaint on whether she touched him, what was said beforehand, and who threw a chair in the first place. Both accounts are now shown precisely as what they are: competing sworn statements, not a settled record.

Era One
The Ejection
March 25, 2025

Keen is a Martinsville City Sheriff's Office deputy, assigned to provide security at city council meetings — under Sheriff Steve Draper, a separate office from the Martinsville Police Department. With the office since 2008, she removed Council Member Aaron Rawls from chambers during a council meeting on March 25, 2025. Two sworn accounts of what happened now exist: Rawls's federal complaint, filed June 2, 2025, and Keen's own declaration, filed July 23, 2025 as part of the same case. They do not agree.

Two Sworn Accounts — Directly Contradictory

Rawls's complaint: by the time Keen reached his chair, his exchange with Jones had ended and another council member had begun speaking; he was not attempting to continue speaking or interrupt anyone. Keen physically seized him, required him to leave his chair, and told him, "Outside. Go on outside." No one verbally directed her — she acted on a nonverbal signal from Ferrell-Benavides alone, which the complaint characterizes as a "pre-conceived plan" between the two of them.

Keen's declaration: before the meeting, Jones told her she could "do whatever she needed to do." The meeting proceeded normally before devolving into what she called "aggressive and disruptive" remarks from Rawls, including pointed accusations toward Councilman Gravely and members of the public that referenced Gravely's prior criminal charges. Rawls defied repeated requests from Jones to "fall in order," at which point Ferrell-Benavides gave the nonverbal instruction Keen understood as direction to remove him. Keen states plainly: "Based on all of these factors, as a matter of public safety and to maintain order at the City Council meeting, I approached Councilman Rawls and instructed him to leave." She emphasizes Rawls complied voluntarily, that she never physically touched him, and that she escorted him out through a side door — not the main chamber doors.

Both accounts agree Ferrell-Benavides gave a nonverbal signal. They flatly disagree on whether Keen physically touched Rawls, what (if anything) was said to him, and whether Jones had given her advance verbal authorization before the meeting even started.

Who Threw a Chair — A Separate, Unresolved Conflict

Rawls's own sworn affidavit (documented on Jones's and Rawls's pages) states that Jones shook the back of Rawls's chair and then threw an unoccupied chair, in Room 208, before that day's session. Keen's declaration states that HR Director Travis Hodge told her, before the meeting, that Rawls had thrown a chair. These are two different people accused of throwing a chair, from two different sworn-adjacent sources, and this record is not in a position to resolve which is accurate — both are presented exactly as what they are, competing accounts relayed through different witnesses.

Rawls's complaint also notes that minutes after his removal, Ferrell-Benavides spoke to the remaining council, praising her own performance and stating she was underpaid relative to peer city managers — a moment Rawls says he wasn't present for, since he had already been escorted out.

Era Two
The Federal Defendant
June 2025 – Present

Rawls filed suit against Keen and Ferrell-Benavides, both in their personal capacities, on June 2, 2025, in the U.S. District Court for the Western District of Virginia, Danville Division — Case 4:25-cv-00028. The complaint brings four counts under 42 U.S.C. §1983: First Amendment viewpoint discrimination, Fourth Amendment unreasonable seizure, deprivation of liberty, and Fourteenth Amendment equal protection.

The Signal — No Longer Fully Disputed

Rawls's position: Ferrell-Benavides gave Keen a non-verbal signal, visible on video, directing the removal — part of a coordinated plan between the two of them.

Ferrell-Benavides's position (response filed July 2025): Her legal team has formally denied that any such signal was given.

Keen's position (declaration filed July 23, 2025): Keen's own account confirms a nonverbal instruction from Ferrell-Benavides did occur and that she understood it as direction to remove Rawls — though Keen frames it as one factor among several (Rawls's "aggressive and disruptive" conduct, his defiance of repeated requests to fall in order), not as part of a coordinated plan. Keen's account does not address whether any coordination existed before the meeting; it only describes the moment itself.

Keen has also been subpoenaed to testify in the now-dismissed CL26-011 removal proceeding, in connection with the broader events of that period. Separately, her declaration documents a procedural near-miss: she states she was served the lawsuit June 6, 2025, informed Sheriff Steve Draper, and then received a Clerk's Entry of Default in her mailbox upon returning from the Independence Day holiday — meaning a default judgment was nearly entered against her before she was formally appointed legal counsel on July 10, 2025.

What The Record Shows

Keen is a named defendant in active federal litigation over a specific, video-recorded incident — that much is fact, and she has now given her own sworn account of it. What remains genuinely unresolved is not whether the incident happened, but several specific factual details within it: whether she touched Rawls, what was said, who threw a chair, and whether her authority that night came only from an in-the-moment signal or from advance permission Jones gave her beforehand. This record presents both sworn accounts side by side rather than resolving the conflict between them.
