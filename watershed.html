The Watershed

Forest Park Country Club & Lake Lanier — Martinsville, Virginia

An 85-year-old design feature, not a discrimination lawsuit. In 1938, a planner who later coined the term "urban sprawl" was hired to design a 2,000-acre development for DuPont's management and employees — with lake access built in from the start. Nineteen years later, fifty unidentified graves were moved off the land four months before the club that controls that lake access was incorporated. This page documents the land, the design, the construction, and the corporate structure that followed — and the much older regional history underneath all of it.

Part One
The Land
1730s – 1925

Patrick Henry owned roughly 13,879 acres in this area between 1780 and 1784. The land later passed through Daniel Lanier — the man the lake now bears the name of — to Henry Clay Lester, then to his adopted son, Rives S. Brown Sr. In 1925, Brown formed the Mulberry Development Company, the entity that would eventually shape the land into the subdivisions and lake that exist today.

Both "Mulberry Road" and "Mulberry Court" appear on the modern Lake Lanier Association's own eligibility list — a direct, unbroken naming thread from Brown's 1925 development company to the streets allowed lake access a century later.

Part Two
The Design
1938

In 1938, the Mulberry Development Company hired Earle S. Draper — a nationally prominent planner who had worked under John Nolen and would later coin the term "urban sprawl" — to design roughly 2,000 acres of the Brown/Lanier land into what became the Forest Park and Druid Hills subdivisions. The development was explicitly designed for DuPont's management and employee housing, and lake access was built in from the outset as a designed amenity, not added later.

Draper's prior commission is its own thread in this same network: Stoneleigh, the estate of Thomas B. Stanley — later Governor of Virginia — and his wife Anne Pocahontas Bassett, of the Bassett furniture family. The same planner who designed Stoneleigh for the Stanley-Bassett family went on to design the DuPont-era development that became Forest Park, connecting the Bassett family, the Stanleys, Draper, and the Brown/Lanier Farm network into a single, traceable planning lineage.

Part Three
The Construction & The Club
1955 – Present

1955–1957: The Lake, the Graves, and the Incorporation

Lake Lanier was physically constructed and excavated in 1955–56, per the Rives S. Brown family's own published account. On March 12, 1957, fifty unidentified graves were discovered during construction and moved to a marker near the spillway. The marker's inscription, confirmed via the Martinsville Bulletin, reads plainly:

"Here lie fifty unidentified dead moved from a point 300 yards northeast Mar. 12, 1957"
— Grave marker near the Lake Lanier spillway, per Martinsville Bulletin reporting

Henry County Archivist Desmond Kendrick, on record with the Bulletin, considers Native American origin unlikely for these graves. His leading theory: enslaved or freed people connected to the Hairston family's nearby Mulberry Creek operations — the same Hairston family detailed in Part Four below.

On July 3, 1957 — four months after the graves were moved — Forest Park Country Club was formally incorporated. That timing is treated here as significant, not coincidental: the corporate entity that would govern access to this land was created within the same year, and the same general window, as the unmarked dead were relocated off it.

1960–Present: Expansion and Corporate Structure

In 1960, the club financed a nine-hole expansion through $60,000 in first-mortgage bonds sold directly to the membership, under then-president George Nease. The golf course itself is physically traversed by Mulberry Creek and is accessed via the Spruce Street exit off the US 58 Bypass — the same Spruce Street excluded from the modern Lake Lanier Association's eligibility list, detailed below.

Holding Two Things at Once

A genuine counter-argument exists and deserves to stay visible: Lake Lanier Association membership is street-based, tied to a mailing list and dues, not explicitly race-based on its face, and people of multiple races live on streets both included and excluded from the list. That counter-argument doesn't change the historical record above — the 1938 design-for-DuPont-housing plan, the 1957 grave relocation, and the incorporation timing are all documented fact regardless of how today's list is enforced or perceived. But the present-day enforcement question and the 85-year historical design question are two different things, and this page deliberately keeps them separate. The present-day political dimension of this geography — including its connection to recent Martinsville governance — is documented elsewhere on this Record, on Jennifer Bowles's and LC Jones's own pages, not here.

What The Record Shows

The club that controls physical access to this stretch of land is incorporated, regulated, and leaves a documented paper trail — SCC filings, IRS 990s, named officers. The Association that actually decides who gets to use the lake itself has none of that. One layer of this system is accountable on paper; the other isn't accountable on paper at all.

Part Four
The Regional Watershed
1788 – 1952

Forest Park and Lake Lanier sit inside a much larger regional history along the same Smith River watershed — one that connects several of the area's most prominent family names to a shared economic foundation.

Hairston

Samuel Hairston (1788–1875) was possibly the wealthiest man in America in 1854, enslaving approximately 1,700 people across Henry and Patrick Counties, Virginia, and Stokes County, North Carolina; the broader Hairston family enslaved an estimated 10,000 people total across generations. Hairston-named places remain on the map today: Chatmoss (now a country club, on the site of a burned Hairston house), Leatherwood, and Magna Vista (the site of the former Marrowbone plantation). The Henry County Courthouse itself was built on Hairston-donated land. George Hairston founded the Smith River Navigation Company — the same river system that fed both Bassett's timber operations and the Forest Park golf course.

Bassett

John D. Bassett founded Bassett Furniture Company in 1902, built from Smith River timber; the family donated land for the railroad, and the town of Bassett is named for them. The same family connects to Forest Park through the Draper/Stoneleigh/Stanley thread documented in Part Two.

Philpott

Philpott Dam, built by the Army Corps of Engineers between 1948 and 1952, required the exhumation and reinterment of 29 cemeteries in 1950, displacing multiple families from the land that became Philpott Lake. Fayerdale — a lost mining, timber, and moonshine town — now sits beneath that same lake.

What The Record Shows — Part Four

None of this regional history is offered as proof of anything about the present-day Lake Lanier dispute specifically. It documents that the land underneath this entire area — Forest Park, the Smith River corridor, Philpott Lake — has a real, repeated pattern of large land and water projects displacing people, often without their names being recorded. The 1957 unidentified graves at Lake Lanier are one instance of a pattern that repeats at Philpott five years earlier. That pattern is the actual subject of this page.
