Person Record · Full Career

Charles Roark

Founder, Cable 6 / Star News TV — 1990 to Present

A felony theft conviction, prison time, and a few thousand saved dollars built the station that has since covered every figure in this record. Thirty-five years before any of them held office, Roark was already telling people exactly who he was: "my background's not too good, but here I am." This page documents the full arc — not to discredit the coverage, but because the man behind the camera has his own extensively documented record, and this Record holds everyone to the same standard.

Era One
The Origin
1985 – 1990

Before Cable 6 existed, Roark had already been convicted of theft four times across multiple Virginia jurisdictions. In December 1985, he was convicted in Campbell County of stealing $35 worth of property from a Brookneal lumber company — a suspended 10-day jail term, a $100 fine, and restitution. Less than a week later, he was arrested at a Madison Heights Kroger for taking videotapes, pleading guilty to shoplifting in January 1986. In October 1992, he was convicted of stealing two Martinsville Bulletin newspapers from a coin-operated rack — fined $300, given a suspended three-month jail term, with a separate resisting-arrest charge dismissed after video evidence showed he hadn't been given a fair chance to set down his camera peacefully.

The most serious conviction was a felony: grand larceny for stealing $100,000 in television equipment from a station in Asheville, North Carolina. Roark served time for it.

"I'm Charles Roark and my background's not too good, but here I am."
— Charles Roark, on starting Cable 6, as recounted in a 1994 newspaper profile

He came home to Martinsville in 1990 and started the town's first cable-access TV station with a few thousand dollars he'd saved — a one-man operation at first. By 1994, four years in, Cable 6 had grown to nine full-time staff and reached an estimated 19,000 households across Henry County and Martinsville from its base in Collinsville.

What The Record Shows

Roark has never hidden this history — he stated it himself, in his own words, at the very start of his career. The pattern is documented across multiple independent jurisdictions and time periods, not a single incident. It is presented here factually, the same way this Record documents every other figure's history, positive or negative.

Era Two
The Cable Wars and the Verdict
1994 – 2005

The Channel 57 Rivalry

In May 1994, a rival operation — Channel 57 (W57BZ), backed by Southern Broadcasting Corp. and businessman Bill Wyatt — launched as a self-described "community-oriented" alternative to Cable 6's reputation for sensationalism. The two stations spent the following months digging into each other's staff. Cable 6 exposed an affair between Wyatt and a Channel 57 saleswoman, Ramona Hines, who lost her job as a result; Channel 57 in turn investigated a Cable 6 on-air contributor's own legal troubles.

"Like it or not, smut sells."
— Charles Roark, on his own approach, 1994

Henry County Sheriff Frank Cassell, a frequent target of Cable 6's coverage, was blunt about the toll it took on his department.

"The No. 1 problem we have in Henry County is drugs, and No. 2 is Cable 6."
— Sheriff Frank Cassell, 1994

Roark maintained the station had never been sued over this period, attributing it to a willingness to air corrections. "We admit when we do something wrong, and we don't have a problem with it," he said at the time.

The Ziglar Defamation Judgment

That claim didn't hold indefinitely. In February 2002, Martinsville Commonwealth's Attorney Joan Ziglar sued Roark and his company — by then operating as Media 6 — for $3 million, after his tabloid publication, Buzz, printed a letter from a jailed murder suspect accusing her of encouraging a witness to lie to a grand jury and alleging an affair with his sister. At trial, Ziglar's sister testified there had been no such relationship, and the underlying accusation was never substantiated.

What The Record Shows

A Roanoke jury found in Ziglar's favor and awarded her $75,000 — far less than the $3 million sought, but a real, adjudicated defamation judgment against Roark's operation, not just an allegation. Buzz ceased publication in 2002. Roark's defense — "it's not our words, it's their words" — describes routine practice for any outlet that quotes inmates or anonymous sources: it does not exempt an outlet from liability for defamatory content it chooses to publish.

Era Three
Star News Today
Present

Cable 6 evolved through Media 6 and Cable 18 into Star News TV, operating today as Star News Corp. Roark remains the central on-camera figure, with Star News Creative Services crediting editors Doug Talbot and Jonathan Herring. The station's modern-era incidents involving Roark himself — the 2023 Northview phone confrontation (Henry County Sheriff's Office jurisdiction) and the 2024 Tequila's assault (Martinsville Police Department) — are documented in full on Robert Fincher's person-page, though only the 2024 incident actually intersects with Fincher's own department; the 2023 incident fell under a different agency's jurisdiction entirely.

A Documented Pattern of Overlap

Star News's role in this record isn't limited to neutral coverage. Vice Mayor Kathy Lawson was photographed standing with Roark at a Star News-covered Christmas Parade in October 2025 — the same month Rico Johnson's lawsuit against Fincher and Rawls was filed. Separately, Star News has been the platform of record for several contested moments already documented elsewhere in this Record — Krudys's public statements blaming Rawls, Jennifer Bowles's post-hearing commentary, and the "fake page" labeling pattern documented in Chapter 6.

Open Question

Star News has at various points described itself publicly as the region's leading news source. Whether that claim reflects an independently verifiable audience metric or is simply self-characterization has not been confirmed either way and should not be repeated as fact without that verification.

What The Record Shows

Star News TV has been a genuine public service in this community for over three decades, and its coverage is cited extensively throughout this entire Record as primary source material — that value is real and shouldn't be minimized. It has also operated, at points in its history, with a documented defamation judgment against it and a founder with an extensive prior theft record he disclosed himself. Both things are true. Readers relying on Star News as a source — as this Record does, constantly — should have both in view.
